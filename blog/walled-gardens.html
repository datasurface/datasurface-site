The Walled Gardens Are Back (And This Time It’s Your Data)

November 18, 2025 • By Billy Newport

Many of us are old enough to remember the "internet" of the early 90s. CompuServe, AOL, and Prodigy dominated—each a walled garden with proprietary apps, dial-up modems, and captive customers. You lived entirely within your provider’s world, isolated from users of other services except for email.

That world seemed permanent. Until it wasn’t.

Then HTTP Changed Everything

HTTP and Netscape arrived and suddenly anyone could build websites on the open internet. The walled gardens, seeing the writing on the wall, grudgingly allowed their customers to use PPP—a protocol to access the broader internet through their modem banks.

Within a few years, there was more content on the open web than inside all the walled gardens combined. The rest is history.

We’re Living Through This Again (But With Data)

Today's data landscape looks eerily like the 90s internet. Walled gardens run by Big Data Vendors want you to move your data into their technology and pay them to control access to it forever.

Just like CompuServe customers in 1994, many companies don’t realize they’re building on someone else’s proprietary platform. When that technology inevitably changes, migrating will be very expensive or impossible.

I believe we’re about to enter a new data era.

DataSurface: The HTTP for Data

Just as internet users today don’t care how packets traverse the network, DataSurface users won’t care how data moves from producers to consumers.

Three Types of Data Citizens

DataSurface defines a collaborative model built around three roles:

  • Data Producers: "I have data that others need, but I want to control who uses it and for what purpose."
  • Data Consumers: "I need data from others in a container that fits my use case. I may need the data with low latency; I need 5 year retention for regulatory requirements; I want to query the data in a transactional way/ a reporting way/ an analytical way."
  • Data Transformers: "I want to create value-added data using the right technology for my transformation."

These actors collaborate through a shared model. The model evolves over time, but DataSurface enforces strict rules ensuring model changes respect:

  • Who can change what: Full audit trails for all model changes.
  • Backwards compatibility: No breaking changes.
  • Technology limitations: Only use technology that is trusted for purpose.
  • Location requirements: Respect data residency laws/policies.
  • Vendor constraints: Certain types of data are only allowed on specific vendor technologies.
  • Governance boundaries: Honor organizational policies.
  • Purpose controls: Enforce usage restrictions.

The model defines what should happen. DataSurface figures out how to make it happen using the best available technology—and that technology can change over time without impacting the model’s users. Change the model and minutes later data is flowing for new use cases.

No walled gardens. No lock-in. High velocity.
